## 1. Executive Summary

The GIS and geolocation intelligence SaaS market is divided into four major competitive clusters:

1. **Enterprise GIS platforms**  
   Examples: Esri ArcGIS, Precisely / MapInfo  
   These platforms offer deep GIS capabilities, strong enterprise trust, and mature public-sector adoption, but they are often complex and expensive.

2. **Developer-first maps and location APIs**  
   Examples: Google Maps Platform, Mapbox, HERE, TomTom, Radar, Geoapify  
   These companies focus on maps, routing, geocoding, search, navigation, and location infrastructure for applications.

3. **Cloud-native spatial analytics platforms**  
   Examples: CARTO, Felt, Atlas  
   These platforms focus on browser-based GIS, collaboration, spatial analytics, BI workflows, and easier adoption for modern teams.

4. **Location data providers**  
   Examples: Foursquare, SafeGraph  
   These companies provide POI data, foot traffic data, place intelligence, and commercial location datasets.

The strongest opportunity for a new entrant is not to compete directly with Esri or Google Maps. A better strategy is to build a **verticalized, workflow-first location intelligence product** for a specific business decision.

Potential opportunity areas include:

- Real estate site risk intelligence
- Retail store expansion scoring
- Logistics coverage and delivery-zone planning
- Insurance and geohazard underwriting
- Telecom network and coverage planning
- Urban planning dashboards for local governments

The key market gap is that many existing tools are either:

- Too technical for business users, or
- Too generic for high-value business decisions

A new entrant can differentiate by combining geospatial data, local datasets, simple decision workflows, explainable scoring, and industry-specific use cases.

## 2. Market Overview

GIS and geolocation SaaS products generally solve five types of problems:

| Problem Type | Example Use Cases | Buyer / User |
|---|---|---|
| Mapping and visualization | Dashboards, thematic maps, asset maps | Analysts, planners, operations teams |
| Spatial analysis | Buffers, catchments, drive-time, clustering, risk zones | GIS analysts, data teams |
| Location APIs | Maps, geocoding, routing, autocomplete, navigation | Developers, product teams |
| Location intelligence | Site selection, trade area analysis, competitive analysis | Retail, real estate, strategy teams |
| Location data | POI, foot traffic, boundaries, demographics | Data science, BI, enterprise analytics |

### Key Market Trends

#### 1. Cloud-native GIS is replacing desktop-only workflows

Traditional GIS workflows are moving from desktop software into web-based and collaborative platforms.

Examples:

- Felt focuses on browser-based collaborative GIS.
- Atlas positions itself as AI-native GIS.
- CARTO focuses on cloud-native spatial analytics.

#### 2. Maps APIs are becoming infrastructure

Maps, routes, search, geocoding, and location services are increasingly treated as infrastructure for modern applications.

Examples:

- Google Maps Platform
- Mapbox
- HERE
- TomTom
- Radar
- Geoapify

#### 3. AI is becoming a new positioning layer

AI is increasingly used to simplify geospatial workflows, help users query data, generate insights, and reduce dependence on GIS specialists.

Examples:

- Atlas uses AI-native GIS positioning.
- Google Maps Platform is adding AI-related tools and geospatial intelligence features.
- CARTO supports advanced spatial analytics and data science workflows.

---

## 7.2 Pricing Lessons for a New Entrant

A new entrant should avoid complex GIS-style pricing at the start.

Recommended packaging:

| Plan | Target Customer | Packaging Idea |
|---|---|---|
| Free / Trial | Individual evaluator | Limited reports, sample geography, watermark |
| Starter | SMB, founder, consultant | Fixed monthly price, limited projects and exports |
| Team | Small business, agency | Multiple users, collaboration, saved templates |
| Professional | Real estate, retail, logistics team | Advanced datasets, scoring, exports, API access |
| Enterprise | Government, large enterprise | SSO, audit logs, custom data, SLA, private deployment |

For Indonesia or Southeast Asia, a strong pricing wedge could be **report-based pricing** instead of pure SaaS seats.

Example assumptions:

- Rp99.000–Rp299.000 per location report for consumers or agents
- Rp1.000.000–Rp5.000.000 per month for small property, retail, or logistics teams
- Custom enterprise pricing for developers, banks, insurers, governments, or property developers

These are assumptions and need market validation through buyer interviews.

# 10. Final Recommendations

## Recommendation 1 — Do Not Build a Generic GIS Platform

Competing directly with Esri, Google, Mapbox, or HERE is structurally difficult.

They already own:

- Infrastructure layers
- Developer ecosystems
- Enterprise trust
- Data partnerships
- Brand recognition

A new entrant should use existing maps and data infrastructure, then compete on:

- Workflow
- Data interpretation
- Vertical UX
- Business decision support

---

## Recommendation 2 — Pick One Painful Location Decision

The best initial wedges are:

1. Real estate location risk report
2. Retail site selection scoring
3. Logistics coverage planning
4. Insurance or geohazard risk API

The strongest first wedge is:

> Real estate location risk intelligence for first-time homebuyers and property teams.

Reason:

- Easier to validate with concierge reports
- Does not require building full GIS infrastructure first
- Pain is understandable to non-technical buyers
- Strong fit with pre-DP decision moments

---

## Recommendation 3 — Differentiate Through Explainability, Not Just Maps

The product should not only show layers or map points.

It should explain:

- What risk exists
- Why it matters
- How confident the data is
- What action the user should take
- What needs to be verified manually during a site survey

This is where incumbents are weaker for non-GIS business users.

---

## Recommendation 4 — Start as a Concierge MVP Before SaaS

Suggested MVP path:

| Stage | Product Form | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Stage 1 | Manual or semi-manual PDF reports | Validate willingness to pay |
| Stage 2 | Internal dashboard and report generator | Improve delivery speed |
| Stage 3 | Self-serve web application | Scale acquisition |
| Stage 4 | API or B2B dashboard | Expand to property, banking, insurance, and developer partners |

---

## Recommendation 5 — Position Around Confidence-Based Location Decisions

Recommended positioning statement:

> We help teams evaluate location risk and opportunity using explainable geospatial intelligence, so they can make better property, expansion, and operational decisions without needing a GIS specialist.

This avoids competing directly on map infrastructure and creates a clearer business-value narrative.

---

# Strategic Takeaway

The GIS and geolocation SaaS market is mature at the infrastructure level but still has gaps at the decision-support level.

A new entrant should avoid building another generic map or GIS tool.

The better opportunity is to build a verticalized location intelligence product that translates geospatial data into practical decisions for a specific audience.

For Rumper or a similar new product, the best early positioning is:

> Buyer-side location risk intelligence for residential property decisions.

This creates differentiation from:

- Property portals
- Real estate agents
- Generic GIS platforms
- Mapping APIs
- Home inspection services
- Enterprise location intelligence platforms

The strategic focus should be:

1. Start with one high-pain vertical.
2. Use existing maps and public data.
3. Deliver explainable reports.
4. Validate willingness to pay manually.
5. Scale into SaaS only after the workflow is proven.
